# The Family Safe Word Protocol: A Simple Trick That Stops Scammers Cold

Last month, Robert and Carol S. from Dayton, Ohio almost sent $5,000 to a scammer pretending to be their grandson. The voice on the phone sounded exactly like him. The panic in his voice was convincing. The story about needing bail money was urgent.

But Robert paused and asked one question: "What's the word?"

Silence. Then a stumble. Then a hang-up.

That one question saved Robert and Carol $5,000 -- and possibly their retirement savings. The "word" was their family safe word, a secret phrase that only their real family members know. No scammer, no matter how advanced their AI, can guess it.

## What Is a Family Safe Word?

A family safe word is a simple, pre-agreed phrase that your family uses to verify identity during suspicious phone calls, texts, or video calls. Think of it as a password for your family.

It works because AI voice cloning and deepfake technology can replicate how someone sounds, but they cannot replicate what someone knows. A scammer can copy your grandson's voice from a 30-second Instagram clip. They cannot copy a private word your family chose over dinner.

## How to Set Up Your Family Safe Word in 5 Minutes

### Step 1: Choose Your Word

Pick something that is:

- **Easy to remember** but **hard to guess**. Avoid birthdays, pet names, or anything on social media.
- **Not a single common word**. "Banana" is too simple. "Purple mountain thunder" is better.
- **Fun or meaningful to your family**. Inside jokes work great. The sillier, the more memorable.

Good examples:
- "Grandpa's fishing hat"
- "Pineapple on Tuesday"
- "Red boots at the lake"

Bad examples:
- Your pet's name
- A birthday or anniversary
- "Password" or "safe word"

### Step 2: Share It In Person

Never share your safe word over text, email, or phone. Those channels can be intercepted. Share it:

- At a family dinner
- During a holiday gathering
- In a private, face-to-face conversation

Make sure every family member who might receive a suspicious call knows the word. This includes grandparents, parents, adult children, and teenagers.

### Step 3: Practice Using It

Run a practice drill. Have a family member call and pretend to ask for money. The person answering should ask for the safe word before agreeing to anything.

This might feel awkward the first time. That is normal. After one or two practice runs, it becomes second nature.

### Step 4: Set the Rules

Agree on these rules as a family:

1. **If someone calls asking for money or personal information, always ask for the safe word first.**
2. **If they cannot give the safe word, hang up immediately.** No exceptions.
3. **If they get upset or pressure you to skip the safe word, that is a red flag.** Real family members will understand.
4. **Call the person back on their known number** to verify the situation after hanging up.

### Step 5: Update It Periodically

Change your safe word every 6 to 12 months, or immediately if you suspect it has been compromised. Share the new word in person, just like the first time.

## Why This Works Against AI Scams

Modern AI scams are sophisticated. Scammers can now:

- Clone someone's voice from a few seconds of audio
- Create deepfake video calls that look like real people
- Spoof caller ID to show your family member's phone number
- Generate convincing text messages in someone's writing style

But all of these attacks share one weakness: they replicate appearance, not knowledge. A safe word exploits this weakness perfectly.

When you ask "What's the word?" you are asking for something the AI was never trained on. The scammer has no data to work with. The attack falls apart.

## What to Do If You Get a Suspicious Call

1. **Stay calm.** Scammers use urgency and panic to override your judgment.
2. **Ask for the safe word.** If they cannot provide it, the call is fake.
3. **Hang up.** Do not engage further.
4. **Call your family member directly** on their known phone number.
5. **Report the call** to the FTC at reportfraud.ftc.gov and to your local police.
