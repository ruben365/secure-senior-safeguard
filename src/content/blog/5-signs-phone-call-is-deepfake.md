# 5 Signs a Phone Call Is a Deepfake

You pick up the phone. It sounds like your grandson. He says he's in trouble and needs money right now. Your heart races. But something feels off.

That call might not be real. In 2026, scammers use artificial intelligence to copy someone's voice and make phone calls that sound almost exactly like a real person. This is called a deepfake phone call, and it's one of the fastest-growing scams in the country.

Here's the good news: deepfake phone calls are not perfect. There are warning signs you can spot if you know what to listen for. Let's walk through five of them together.

## Why Deepfake Phone Calls Are More Common in 2026

A few years ago, creating a fake voice took hours of audio and expensive software. Not anymore. Today, scammers only need a few seconds of someone's voice -- pulled from a social media video, a voicemail greeting, or even a phone call -- to build a convincing copy.

The tools are cheap, easy to find, and getting better fast. The FBI reported a sharp increase in AI voice scams targeting families across the country in 2025, and the numbers keep climbing. Right here in the Dayton and Kettering area, we've heard from families who received these calls.

That's why every family needs to know the warning signs.

## 1. Unusual Pauses or Robotic Cadence

Real conversations have a natural flow. People overlap each other, laugh at odd moments, and change their tone mid-sentence. AI-generated voices struggle with this.

During a deepfake phone call, you might notice:

- **Odd pauses** between sentences, as if the caller is waiting for the software to catch up
- **Flat or mechanical rhythm** that sounds rehearsed, like someone reading from a script
- **Delayed reactions** to your questions, especially if you interrupt or change the subject

If the person on the other end sounds like they're reading lines instead of talking to you, pay attention. That unnatural rhythm is one of the strongest signs of a deepfake.

**What to do:** Pause the conversation. Ask an unexpected question. A real person will react naturally. A deepfake often stumbles.

## 2. Emotional Urgency Pushing You to Act Fast

Scammers know that when you're scared or upset, you stop thinking clearly. That's why almost every deepfake phone call comes with a crisis.

"Mom, I was in an accident."
"Grandpa, I'm in jail. Please don't tell anyone."
"I need you to send money right now or something terrible will happen."

The caller pushes you to act immediately. They don't want you to stop and think. They don't want you to hang up and call the real person. They want your money before you realize the voice isn't real.

Real emergencies deserve a calm response. No matter how scary the call sounds, you have time to verify. Hang up and call your family member directly using a number you already have saved. If they're truly in trouble, they'll answer or call you back.

**What to do:** Take a breath. Tell the caller you'll call them right back. Then call your loved one on a number you trust.

## 3. The Voice Sounds "Close but Off" from the Real Person

This is the one that trips up the most people. A deepfake voice can sound remarkably similar to someone you love. But "similar" is not "exact."

Listen carefully for:

- **Slight changes in pitch** that don't match how your loved one normally speaks
- **Missing verbal habits** -- maybe your daughter always says "um" or your son clears his throat before speaking, and the caller doesn't
- **Wrong emotional tone** -- the words say "I'm scared," but the voice sounds flat or calm
- **A quality that's hard to name** -- something just feels wrong, even if you can't explain why

Trust your gut. You know your family's voices better than any software does. If something sounds off, it probably is.

**What to do:** Don't ignore that feeling. If the voice doesn't sit right with you, treat the call as suspicious until you can verify it another way.

## 4. The Caller Can't Answer Personal Questions Naturally

Here's where deepfake phone calls fall apart. The AI can copy a voice, but it doesn't know your family's inside jokes, memories, or daily routines.

Try asking something only the real person would know:

- "What did we have for dinner last Sunday?"
- "What's the name of your dog?"
- "What did you get me for my birthday?"

A real family member will answer without hesitation. A scammer -- even one using a perfect voice clone -- will dodge the question, give a vague answer, or try to change the subject.

This is also where **family safe words** come in. A safe word is a secret word or phrase that your family agrees on ahead of time. It's something only your family knows. If someone calls claiming to be a relative, ask for the safe word. If they can't give it, hang up.

Pick a safe word that's easy to remember but hard to guess. Don't use birthdays, pet names, or anything posted on social media. Something silly works great -- "purple dinosaur" or "banana pancakes." The stranger it is, the safer it is.

**What to do:** Set up a family safe word this week. Practice it with your family so everyone remembers. It takes two minutes and could save thousands of dollars.

## 5. Background Noise Is Missing or Artificially Consistent

Think about the last time you got a real phone call from someone out and about. You probably heard traffic, wind, other people talking, or the echo of a room.

Deepfake phone calls often have one of two problems with background noise:

- **Dead silence** -- no ambient sound at all, which feels unnatural for a call from "the side of the road" or "a police station"
- **Perfectly looping noise** -- the same sounds repeating in an exact pattern, like a short recording playing on repeat

Real life is messy. Real phone calls pick up unexpected sounds. If the background is too clean or too consistent, that's a red flag.

**What to do:** Listen to the whole call, not just the voice. If the background doesn't match the story the caller is telling, be suspicious.

## What to Do if You Suspect a Deepfake Phone Call

If you notice any of these signs, here's your game plan:

1. **Stay calm.** The scammer wants you panicked. Don't give them that power.
2. **Don't send money.** No matter what the caller says, do not wire money, buy gift cards, or share banking information during the call.
3. **Hang up and verify.** Call your loved one directly using a number from your contacts. If you can't reach them, call another family member who can check.
4. **Use your family safe word.** If you stay on the line, ask for it. No safe word, no trust.
5. **Report it.** File a report with the FTC at [reportfraud.ftc.gov](https://reportfraud.ftc.gov) and let your local police know.

You don't have to face this alone. Scammers count on people being too embarrassed to talk about it. Talk about it anyway. Warn your neighbors, your church group, your book club.

## Protect Your Family Before the Call Comes

The best time to prepare for a deepfake phone call is before it happens. Set up your family safe word. Talk to your parents and grandparents about these warning signs. Make a plan so everyone knows what to do.
