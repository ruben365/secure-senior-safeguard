# 5 Cybersecurity Tips Every Ohio Small Business Needs in 2026

If you run a small business in Ohio, you are a target. Not because you are doing anything wrong, but because small businesses are the easiest prey for cybercriminals.

Here is the reality: 43% of cyberattacks target small businesses, and 60% of small businesses that suffer a major breach close within six months. In 2026, AI has made these attacks faster, cheaper, and harder to detect.

The good news is that most attacks are preventable with basic security practices. Here are five things you can do this week to dramatically reduce your risk.

## 1. Train Your Team to Spot AI-Powered Phishing

Phishing is no longer just badly spelled emails from fake princes. In 2026, attackers use AI to craft messages that look identical to emails from your bank, your vendors, or even your own CEO.

**What AI phishing looks like now:**
- Perfect grammar and formatting that mirrors your actual business communications
- Spoofed email addresses that are one character different from real ones
- Urgent requests for wire transfers or password resets that reference real projects
- Follow-up emails if you do not respond to the first one

**What to do:**
- Run quarterly phishing simulation training for all employees
- Establish a verification protocol for any financial request over $500
- Use a secondary channel (phone call or in-person) to confirm unusual requests
- Never click links in unexpected emails -- navigate to the website directly instead

## 2. Enable Multi-Factor Authentication Everywhere

If your business accounts are protected by passwords alone, you are one data breach away from losing everything. Multi-factor authentication (MFA) adds a second layer -- usually a code sent to your phone -- that stops attackers even if they steal your password.

**Where to enable MFA immediately:**
- Email accounts (Gmail, Outlook, Yahoo)
- Banking and financial platforms
- Cloud storage (Google Drive, Dropbox, OneDrive)
- Social media accounts
- Your website admin panel
- Payroll and HR systems

**Pro tip:** Use an authenticator app (Google Authenticator, Microsoft Authenticator, or Authy) instead of SMS codes. SIM-swapping attacks can intercept text messages, but authenticator apps are much harder to compromise.

## 3. Back Up Everything -- And Test Your Backups

Ransomware attacks encrypt your files and demand payment to unlock them. If you have clean backups, you can restore your data without paying a dime.

**The 3-2-1 backup rule:**
- **3** copies of your data
- **2** different storage types (e.g., external hard drive and cloud)
- **1** copy stored offsite or in the cloud

**Critical:** Test your backups regularly. A backup you have never restored is a backup you cannot trust. Schedule a monthly test restore of a random file to make sure your system works.

**What to back up:**
- Customer databases and contact lists
- Financial records and tax documents
- Employee records and contracts
- Website files and databases
- Email archives
- Any proprietary documents or intellectual property

## 4. Secure Your Wi-Fi and Network

Your business Wi-Fi is a front door that most business owners leave wide open. An unsecured network lets attackers intercept customer data, install malware, and access your internal systems.

**Quick wins:**
- Change the default router admin password (most are still "admin/admin")
- Use WPA3 encryption (or WPA2 at minimum)
- Create a separate guest network for customers and visitors
- Hide your business network SSID (network name) from public view
- Update your router firmware quarterly
- Use a VPN for any remote access to business systems

**If you have a retail location:** Your point-of-sale system should be on its own isolated network, separate from your general business Wi-Fi and any customer-facing network. This prevents a compromised guest device from reaching your payment processing.

## 5. Create an Incident Response Plan

Most small businesses discover they need a plan after something goes wrong. By then, the panic and confusion cost you time, money, and customer trust.

**Your plan should answer these questions:**
- Who do you call first? (Your IT contact, your insurance company, law enforcement)
- How do you isolate the affected systems?
- How do you notify customers if their data is compromised?
- Where are your backups and how do you restore from them?
- Who communicates with the media or public?

**Write it down. Print it out. Keep a copy somewhere that does not require a computer to access.**

Your plan does not need to be 50 pages. A simple one-page document with phone numbers, steps, and responsibilities is better than nothing.

## Bonus: Know Your Legal Obligations

Ohio businesses are subject to data breach notification laws. If customer personal information is compromised, you may be legally required to notify affected individuals and the Ohio Attorney General's office. Ignorance is not a defense.

If you handle payment card data, you also need to comply with PCI DSS requirements. A breach that exposes credit card numbers can result in fines, lawsuits, and loss of your ability to process payments.
